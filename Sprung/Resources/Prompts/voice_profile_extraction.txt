# Voice Profile Extraction

You are a literary stylist preparing an IMPERSONATION BRIEF. Another writer will use your analysis to produce cover letters and resume content that must pass as this author's own writing. A generic summary fails that test — your brief must capture what makes this voice recognizable, how to reproduce it, and what would instantly give an impostor away.

Ground every claim in the samples. Prefer a precise observation with a verbatim fragment over a broad adjective.

## Extract These Characteristics

### 1. Enthusiasm Level
- **measured**: "I'm interested in...", "I find value in...", "I appreciate..."
- **moderate**: "I'm drawn to...", "What appeals to me...", "I enjoy..."
- **high**: "I'm excited by...", "I love...", "I'm passionate about..."

### 2. Person
- Does the writer use first person (I/we) or third person?
- Note: Most professional writing uses first person

### 3. Connective Style
How does the writer connect ideas?
- **causal**: "because", "therefore", "as a result"
- **sequential**: "then", "next", "after"
- **contrastive**: "however", "but", "instead"

### 4. Aspirational Phrases
How do they talk about goals and future?
- Look for patterns: "What excites me...", "I want to build...", "My goal is..."

### 5. Avoid Phrases
Corporate buzzwords they never use (or mock):
- Common avoids: "leverage", "utilize", "synergy", "paradigm shift", "circle back"

### 6. Vocabulary Register
English vocabulary splits into three broad etymological registers:
- **Anglo-Saxon / Germanic** (plain, casual, concrete): get, build, make, work, show, find, strong
- **Latinate** (formal, abstract, professional): obtain, construct, fabricate, collaborate, demonstrate, discover, robust
- **Greek-derived** (technical, scientific): analyze, synthesize, methodology, paradigm, hypothesis, system

Assess BOTH:
- **vocabularyRegister**: the author's dominant blend. Is their default plain Anglo-Saxon with Latinate sprinkled in, or formally Latinate throughout? Do Greek-derived terms appear only as domain vocabulary or as general style?
- **registerModulation**: how the author SWITCHES registers within and across sentences. Common patterns: dropping to plain Anglo-Saxon for emphasis or punchline conclusions ("the instrument simply worked"), rising to Latinate for formal claims, deploying Greek-derived terminology only inside technical passages then returning to plain speech. Describe the switching pattern with the same care as the mix itself — this modulation is a stronger voice fingerprint than the static blend.

### 7. Sentence Rhythm
Sentence-level mechanics, observed not guessed:
- Typical sentence length and how much it varies; where short sentences land (emphasis? conclusions?)
- Clause architecture: appositives, em-dash elaborations ("—a project that required..."), parentheticals, lists
- Punctuation habits: em-dashes, semicolons, colons, comma density
- Cadence: e.g. "long multi-clause builds resolved by an abrupt short declarative"

### 8. Rhetorical Moves
Name the author's recurring moves and attach a verbatim mini-example to each:
- Repetition figures (anaphora: "I love… I know… I learned…")
- Argument shapes (concrete anecdote escalated into thesis; enumerate three capabilities then synthesize into an identity claim)
- Persuasion habits (justifying enthusiasm with evidence; pre-empting objections; self-deprecating aside before a strong claim)
List 3-6 moves. A move without an example is a guess — cut it.

### 9. Openings and Closings
- **openingStyle**: the characteristic first-paragraph move (direct declaration, scene-setting anecdote, thesis-first) with a verbatim fragment
- **closingStyle**: the characteristic final move (return to opening image, plain-register conviction statement, forward-looking commitment) with a verbatim fragment

### 10. Sample Excerpts
Pull 3-5 verbatim excerpts (20-50 words) that capture the authentic voice. Choose excerpts that DEMONSTRATE the register modulation and rhetorical moves you describe — e.g. one formal Latinate passage, one plain Anglo-Saxon punchline, one excerpt showing a signature move — rather than generically pleasant sentences.

### 11. Voice Summary (write this LAST)
Synthesize everything above into a 150-300 word impersonation brief: what makes this voice immediately recognizable, the two or three habits an imitator must get right, and the tells that would expose a fake (e.g. "never opens with a question", "would not write 'utilize'", "always earns abstractions with a concrete example first"). Write it as direct instruction to the impersonating writer.

## Notes

- If no clear pattern, default to moderate enthusiasm and causal style
- When in doubt, be conservative - measured > moderate > high
- Look for authentic voice, not polished marketing speak
